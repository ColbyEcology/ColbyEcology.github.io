I'm currently standing in front of you, watching you all take Exam III. Thank you for putting your time and effort into this class, including this final, relatively-large-stakes assessment today. As this will be the last time that we are all in a room together with ecological science as the reason, it makes me reflect on the course and the future.

This course was a course in ecological science. That is, we focused on, as you know, the spatial and temporal patterns of the distributions and abundances of organisms, including the causes and consequences. We took three perspective: populations, communities, and ecosystems. Each perspective approaches ecology in a different way because they are largely at different scales and have different levels and types of complexity.

When I look out at the class, I see the future. To prepare you, the future, I have done my best to equip you with the knowledge about ecology and tools that will hopefully help you in your future endeavors. Personally and professionally, you will most likely interface with ecological problems and have to make decisions that will have ecological impacts. But also, through analogy, you will most certainly will face understanding complex systems, wether biological, economic, or environmental, as examples. I hope many of the concepts and tools that you have learned you can apply to these decisions and problems as well.

I want to thank you for participating in the experimental classroom and format. As I wrote about in the first post on this site, taking a ~50-person class out of the traditional lecture hall into this different setting and experimenting some different teaching techniques was a risk. I think that some things went well; and other things could be improved. For example, I don't know anybody that has tried to have any somewhat-substantive discussion with such a large class. You noticed that I modified the format of the few discussions we had throughout the semester, and I learned a lot. I actually really enjoyed walking around and seing all of you engaged with your paper discussions quite a bit.

I've also really enjoyed getting to know many of you personally. "Student Hours" were relatively highly attended for this course this semester. Opposed to the large class meetings, I much more strongly prefer teaching, discussion, and problem solving in smaller group sizes, like those sizes in Student Hours. Being able to pick your professors' brains and getting instantaneous feedback can really help you learn and undersand! I highly, highly suggest that you all attend all the office hours of professors that you can! Plus, it's part of our job and most and not or grossly under-attended. But also, especially for less-privileged groups (e.g., first-generation students like myself), make note that I am speaking to you! There's been a lot of research finding that under-privileged groups---even at elite colleges (e.g., [link](https://www.theatlantic.com/education/archive/2019/03/privileged-poor-navigating-elite-university-life/585100/)).

You may be leaving this class thinking that ecology is not what you thought: and I think that's a good thing. My approach is to teach more about the science of ecology rather than applications (e.g., human impacts and effects on ecosystems, ecosystem management). Why? Well, because "ecology," as you learn about it in other classes, is 99% applied, and it is important to me that you have a more solid scientific foundation. Thinking about rates of change, variability, complex webs and networks of interactions, understanding diversity, and the movement, sticking, and changing of resources in ecosystems all have strong scientific foundations that will most certainly help you in your other courses and beyond. I hope that you appreciate my chosen approach.

OK, I think that's mostly what I have to say. Please keep in touch, wether you're a second- or third-year student who will be around in the coming years or a fourth-year student who will be leaving! I want to know about all of the amazing things that you will undoubtedly be doing, and I am here for anything ecological or otherwise. Please talk to me especially if you're interested in ecological research.

Again, thank you very, very much.
