> With the utmost sincerity I want to say that I'm absolutely thrilled for this semester's course in Ecology!

Why, you might ask? Because
1. it's an important time in our history to be learning about ecology and
2. I've updated and improved the course structure and content, which includes: 
    - making use of this new classroom
    - working with Colby's Center for Teaching and Learning as a TAL@C Fellow to implement active learning strategies in traditionally lecture-based courses
    - hired a Learning Assistant, Robin Green
    - created one new lab
    - refined all four labs

This will be my third year here at Colby, and the third semester that I'm teaching ecology. As with any endeavor, the course evolves; in our case, I'm applying a strong selective force for a better course! The first two years I focused on the course structure and content, like the number and types of assignments, how much time to allocate to certain topics, which topics to cover in this introductory class, etc. By the end of last year I was fairly satisfied with course structure and content, and this year I will be focusing more on the presentation of material and how I will better engage you with the material.

One of the big changes for me, and likely you, is the space where we'll hold lecture. Classroom settings have evolved and will continue to do so:
![ClassroomHistory](https://media.springernature.com/original/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1007%2Fs10734-014-9742-0/MediaObjects/10734_2014_9742_Fig1_HTML.gif)
([https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10734-014-9742-0](from Park and Choi, 2014)). Right now educators and psychologists are studying how people best learn, including how the classroom environment affects the learning process. Smaller groups learn better on average than larger groups, and larger groups, including our 48-person Ecology class, need more space. It turns out that recent study and thinking suggests that traditional lecture halls may not be the most effective classroom setting. For this reason, Colby built this brand-new room, and we are the first class to use it! For a variety of reasons, a classroom like the one we have should better promote learning compared with traditional lecture halls.

Similarly to the classroom environment, educators and psychologists are making advances in understanding how people better learn, and it turns out that traditional lectures aren't the best. Consequently, the past decade or two, educators have been making efforts to transform the classroom experience from being more passive to more active because—and here's the ringer—people learn when they are engaged! Here's a very impactful study by the author of your BI163/164 text, and other authors, based on a few hundred studies that how that active methods decrease failure:

![ActiveLearningDecreaseFailure](https://www.pnas.org/content/pnas/111/23/8410/F1.large.jpg?width=800&height=600&carousel=1)

and that active methods improves performance in biology courses by about 30% (my eyeball estimate):

![ActiveLearningIncreasesPerformance](https://www.pnas.org/content/pnas/111/23/8410/F2.large.jpg?width=800&height=600&carousel=1)

from [Freeman et al., 2014](https://www.pnas.org/content/111/23/8410).



This will be my third year here at Colby, and the third semester that I will have taught ecology. Year one was focused largely on content; that is, most of my efforts were directed towards disseminating and assessing all of the information that I thought students needed to know about ecology. After a year of experience, year two was a big restructure of the course, including the structure of the course and a new text. This year, year three, I have focused on refining the course material and teaching the material.



Last year the other Ecology professor and I started here at Colby. That means that we both moved here from other parts of the world (he and I moved from Rhode Island and Ohio, respectively) and, with a lot of help from the Lab Instructors, organized and carried out Ecology course. The course was a big experiment: some aspects of the course worked *really* well and others not so well. Well, this fall it's our first opportunity after having significant time to work together (nearly a year) to design this fall's course. We've put a great deal of blood, sweat, and tears into this course to make this the best course in Ecology that we can, which is why I am so thrilled!

This post that you're reading also belongs to another experiment. In Ecology there's consistently great diversity of student interests in this course, with some interested more in policy, some interested more in general biology, and others are testing the waters to see if ecology is a field in which they have interest. These perspectives are highly valued at our liberal arts institution, but it also means that the course material will generally be broad. Because of this, I wanted to create a way to engage those who would like to learn more about ecology in greater depth. To achieve this end, I'll certainly give additional references in lecture material, my office door is always open for further discussion of ecological material, and the book has a great deal of references. However, this semester, I am experimenting with another way of providing material for those who are interested by blogging about ecology. The post contents will likely mostly include news from my lab, news from the department, current events and interesting papers in ecology, or just additional information or elaboration upon a topic in class. The latest 5 blog posts will show up on the home page ([colbyecology.github.io](https://colbyecology.github.io)), and you can discover topics through topics using the search or tag tools in the navigation bag. You can also subscribe to the blog in your RSS feed. tl;dr: I will create posts that are interesting for those who are ecologically oriented.

With that, I'd like to welcome you to Ecology and the fall semester!
