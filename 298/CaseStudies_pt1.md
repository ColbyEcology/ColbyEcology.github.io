# Case Study Presentations, Part 1

## General
The diversity of pathogens is astounding. All major groups of organisms have evolved a pathogenic habits and pathogens and parasites may be the most common habitat across all species!
		
In this course on ecology and evolution of infectious disease, we are largely focusing on biological concepts. In doing so, we will superficially study specific species as they pertain to the biological concept. As examples, we will learn just a little bit about Phonine Distemper Virus when learning about spatial dynamics of disease and will will learn just a little bit about *Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis* (causing the disease, chytridiomycosis).

Beyond learning the biological concepts, I would like for you to deeply learn about the natural history of organisms—especially ones you're interested in. To do so, I invite you to embark on *two* Case Study expeditions where you explore the natural history of a species of interest and share it with the class.

The pathogens on which you choose to focus will be up to the group. I have no parameters for this first set of Case Studies: you can choose pathogens of any organism, including *Homo sapiens*. For the second set, I will ask that you focus on a pathogen that does not infect *Homo sapiens*.

## Presentation Content
Find something that inspires you as a group, and dive into it! I don't have a specific structure for the presentation, but I would like the following information to be sought by you and taught to your peers:

1. **Natural history**
2. **Transmission dynamics**
3. **Evolutionary biology**
4. **Ecological relevance**

Given the extraordinary diversity of pathogens, the details of the biology of each will be different. Nevertheless, here are some guidelines of what to include in each of the aforementioned:

1. The **natural history** of the organism includes the description of its biology. This will include, among other things, the pathogen's lifecycle, geographic distribution, host information, reservoir host (if applicable), transmission pathways, pathogenicity, dormancy, how it causes disease, etc. In sum, this will likely be the bulkiest part of the presentation where you teach us about the most basic aspects of your pathogen's biology.
2. For **transmission dynamics** I would like for you include quantitative popiulation-like information about the pathogen's population characteristics like: time series of incidences (e.g., epidemic/epidenic curves), transmissin rates, recovery rates, latent period, *R*<sub>0</sub>, spatial spread, a decription of its disease model(s), etc.
3. The **evolutionary biology** section will likely be smaller, depending on the pathogen. Here, I think it'd be illuminating to focus on aspects of the pathogen's evolutionary history, including: mutation rates, selection (especially for virulence or anti-biotic resistance), phylogenetic description, etc.
4. For **ecological relevance**, I ask that you share information on how the pathogen affects the host population and effects they have on the larger community or ecosystem.

## Presentation Structure
My main criterion is to make the presentation interesting, so I will allow you creative freedom. I will give each group 15–20 min. for each presentation, with 5 minutes or so for questions.
